Bank Merger Reform — Interagency Scorecard

OCC  ·  FDIC  ·  Federal Reserve  ·  DOJ  ·  Current as of May 2026

The Trump administration moved quickly to reverse Biden-era merger policy, with the OCC and FDIC reinstating pre-2024 standards by May 2025 and the DOJ signaling a more permissive posture. But the picture is incomplete: the Federal Reserve has not updated its framework, and DOJ still operates under the more stringent 2023 Merger Guidelines rather than rejoining the 1995 Bank Merger Guidelines. This scorecard tracks the current posture at each agency and the key unresolved questions.

The Biden-era merger policy regime — characterized by heightened scrutiny, no expedited review, and burdens on applicants to affirmatively demonstrate community benefit — has been substantially reversed at the OCC and FDIC. The Federal Reserve and DOJ remain partial holdouts.

Current posture by agency
OCC
Most permissive reset

Rescinded 2024 policy statement (May 2025). Issued interim final rule reinstating pre-2024 regulations, including the 15-day expedited approval pathway for qualifying transactions. Congressional Review Act resolution signed by President Trump bars reissuance of a substantially similar rule.

Current framework: 1990s-era OCC merger regulations. Supervisory history and CRA record remain relevant factors.

FDIC
Pre-2024 reinstated

Rescinded 2024 Statement of Policy (July 3, 2025; effective August 4, 2025). Reinstated the 1998-era Statement of Policy on Bank Merger Transactions pending a comprehensive future review of merger policy.

Explicitly dropped the Biden-era requirement that applicants affirmatively demonstrate a merger would improve community service more than the status quo — a burden that had effectively frozen application activity.

Federal Reserve
No formal change

The Fed has not issued new merger regulations or policy statements under the Trump administration. It did not join the September 2024 Biden-era changes, nor has it issued updated guidance moving in the opposite direction.

Continues to apply the 1995 Bank Merger Guidelines for competitive analysis. Average review time for deals involving $100B+ firms: 295 days. 413 applications approved in H1 2025.

DOJ
Key holdout

Has not rejoined the 1995 Bank Merger Guidelines withdrawn in September 2024. Continues to apply the 2023 Merger Guidelines with the stricter HHI threshold of 100 points (vs. 200 prior), broader qualitative factors, and no antitrust safe harbors.

Represents the principal remaining source of policy uncertainty — banking agencies and DOJ are now on different analytical frameworks with no formal coordination mechanism.

Treasury Department posture
Political direction
Secretary Bessent has been explicit that merger reform is a priority
In March 2025, Bessent criticized the prior administration's "mission drift," stating that "productive and synergistic mergers are often slowed due to immaterial supervisory issues." He called for refocusing financial regulation on safety and soundness, affordability, and economic growth — providing political cover for a more permissive merger review environment across the interagency.
Key signal: Capital One / Discover approval
Landmark approval
Fed and OCC approved Capital One's acquisition of Discover in April 2025
The approval of the largest bank merger in years — a deal that received over 6,000 public comments and took 295 days to process — signaled regulators are willing to approve complex large-bank transactions where competitive, managerial, CRA, and financial stability factors can be addressed. The Fed acknowledged the DOJ's 1995 Guidelines withdrawal but confirmed it continues to apply the 1995 framework.

Deal activity has accelerated since early 2025. Below are notable transactions that illustrate the current state of merger review — including what approval required and what issues remain live.

Apr 2025 Capital One / Discover Financial Services Approved ▼
Approving agencies: Federal Reserve and OCC (April 2025). FDIC not a primary reviewer.

Deal size: Approximately $35B; creates the largest U.S. credit card issuer by balances.

Key issues addressed: Competitive effects in credit card and payments markets; CRA commitments; managerial capability; financial stability. The deal received over 6,000 public comments and took 295 days to process — the Fed's longest recent review for a major transaction.

Notable: The Fed acknowledged DOJ's withdrawal from the 1995 Guidelines but confirmed it continues to apply the 1995 framework. This was the first major BHC merger approved under the Trump administration and set a template for large-deal review. The deal's approval despite its scale and public comment volume signals the agencies' willingness to clear complex transactions where statutory factors are satisfied.

DOJ posture: No DOJ challenge was filed. Antitrust review coordinated with banking agency process.
2025 pipeline Community bank M&A wave: accelerating deal activity Trend ▼
Context: Industry analysts and practitioners report that Q1 2025 merger activity approached pre-2021 levels following the policy reset. The restoration of expedited review pathways at the OCC and FDIC removed a significant structural barrier that had effectively frozen community bank consolidation since 2024.

Key driver: The 15-day automatic approval pathway for qualifying smaller transactions at the OCC and FDIC meaningfully reduces regulatory risk and deal timeline uncertainty — particularly for community bank transactions where competitive concerns are minimal.

Remaining friction: BSA/AML compliance remains a gatekeeping issue — open MRAs, pending AML enforcement actions, or a weak AML program can block approval regardless of the agency's more permissive posture on other factors. Treasury Sec. Bessent specifically identified AML compliance as an area where supervisory issues were improperly used to block mergers under the prior administration.
H1 2025 Federal Reserve: 413 applications approved in H1 2025 Activity data ▼
Fed application data (H1 2025): The Federal Reserve approved 413 applications in H1 2025 across all application types. Of those, 74 (18%) involved M&A transactions. Nine of twelve applications requiring full Board approval involved M&A; one involved a firm with more than $100 billion in assets (Capital One / Discover).

Processing time: The single $100B+ application took approximately 295 days — consistent with the Fed's average for large complex deals since 2022. Smaller BHC transactions are processed significantly faster at the Reserve Bank level without Board action.

Implication: The Fed's approval volume reflects continued openness to transactions, but its framework has not been formally updated and it retains the longest average processing times for large deals. For transactions requiring Fed approval (all BHC acquisitions), timeline uncertainty remains a planning factor even in the more permissive environment.

The policy reset has cleared considerable uncertainty for smaller and mid-size bank transactions. But for larger or more complex deals, significant interagency tensions and unresolved policy questions remain.

Unresolved tension
Will DOJ rejoin the 1995 Bank Merger Guidelines?
The most consequential remaining misalignment is DOJ's continued use of the stricter 2023 Merger Guidelines (HHI threshold of 100, broader qualitative factors, no antitrust safe harbors) while the banking agencies have reverted to 1995-era standards. The Bank Policy Institute and others have called on DOJ to rejoin the 1995 Guidelines and rescind the 2024 Banking Addendum. Until resolved, applicants face the risk of a post-approval DOJ challenge even if banking agencies approve the transaction. No DOJ action has been announced.
Unresolved tension
Will the Federal Reserve update SR 14-2 and its merger framework?
SR 14-2 — the Fed's supervisory guidance that can block mergers based on less-than-satisfactory supervisory ratings — remains in effect. The Bank Policy Institute has called for its rescission, arguing that only supervisory issues creating a material concern about the merged entity's financial condition should be disqualifying. The Fed has neither rescinded SR 14-2 nor joined the OCC/FDIC policy reset. This is particularly significant because the Fed is the primary regulator for bank holding companies and approves most major merger applications. Its silence on merger reform is the largest remaining structural friction in the system.
Unresolved tension
How will the new AML/CFT program rule affect merger review?
The April 2026 FinCEN/OCC/FDIC/NCUA AML/CFT program rule NPRM — which shifts from technical compliance to effectiveness-based standards — will interact directly with merger review. Strong AML/CFT compliance remains a gatekeeping factor at all agencies. If the new framework reduces the number and severity of AML MRAs, it may indirectly clear a path for transactions that previously faced supervisory obstacles. But the Fed's absence from the joint NPRM raises questions about whether Fed-supervised bank holding companies will benefit on the same timeline.
Watch closely
FDIC comprehensive merger policy review: what comes next?
The FDIC's reinstatement of the 1998 Statement of Policy was explicitly described as an interim step "pending the FDIC's review of all aspects of the regulatory framework governing the evaluation of merger transactions." That comprehensive review has not yet produced a proposal. The 1998 framework is generally viewed favorably by industry, but the FDIC's forthcoming review could introduce new elements — particularly around financial stability review of larger transactions and CRA consideration methodology.
Watch closely
Large-bank merger review: how high does $100B scrutiny now go?
The Biden-era FDIC and OCC policy statements applied categorical heightened scrutiny to transactions producing an institution larger than $100 billion. Those triggers were removed with the policy resets. But the Capital One / Discover approval — the first large-bank test — took 295 days at the Fed. For transactions creating institutions in the $250B–$500B range, the scope of financial stability analysis, FSOC consideration, and political environment remain undefined. This is the frontier question for large-bank consolidation strategy.
Largely settled
Community convenience and needs burden: removed
The Biden-era FDIC requirement that applicants affirmatively demonstrate a proposed merger would better serve community needs than the status quo — with no objective or quantifiable criteria for how FDIC would evaluate this — has been eliminated. This was one of the most criticized features of the 2024 policy and its removal substantially reduces the discretion available to the FDIC to deny applications on vague public interest grounds. Applicants must still satisfy the convenience and needs statutory factor, but the affirmative burden of proof has been removed.
Largely settled
Congressional Review Act lock — 2024 rules cannot simply be re-imposed
President Trump signed a joint CRA resolution invalidating the Biden-era OCC merger rule. Under the CRA, the agency is barred from reissuing a rule that is "substantially the same" as the invalidated rule without express congressional authorization. This provides a structural backstop against a future administration reimposing the 2024 OCC merger framework without legislation — more durable than a simple policy reversal.
