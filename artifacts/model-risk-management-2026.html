What Changed:
The 2026 Revised Model
Risk Management Guidance

A structured breakdown of the 2026 interagency revisions to model risk management — tracking every material shift from SR 11-7, what the agencies added, modified, and left intact, and what it means for AI, machine learning, and algorithmic model governance.

Federal Reserve · OCC · FDIC 2026 Revised Guidance Supersedes SR 11-7 (2011)
Change Key:
NEW — Added in 2026
MODIFIED — Updated from SR 11-7
RETAINED — Core principle unchanged

SR 11-7 has governed model risk management at supervised institutions for over a decade. Issued in 2011, its three-pillar framework — model development and implementation, model validation, and governance — remains the conceptual backbone of the 2026 revision. But the world has changed dramatically.

The proliferation of AI and machine learning models, the rise of third-party and vendor-supplied models, the embedding of models into real-time decisioning systems, and increasing supervisory focus on algorithmic fairness have all strained the original guidance's applicability. The 2026 revision responds — sometimes boldly, sometimes cautiously — to each of these pressures.

This analysis traces every material change, explains what prompted it, and draws out the implications for model risk officers, validators, and the boards and senior leaders who sign off on model risk appetite.

"The definition of a model has not changed. The universe of things that qualify as models — and the risks they carry — has transformed entirely."

— Synthesis of 2026 Guidance Preamble
01

Scope & Definitions

NEW Explicit inclusion of AI/ML models within the model definition ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Defined a model as a quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories to transform inputs into quantitative estimates. AI/ML not addressed; guidance predates widespread ML adoption in banking.

2026 Revision

Explicitly extends the definition to cover AI/ML systems, including large language models used in decisioning, gradient boosting and neural network credit scoring models, and generative AI tools embedded in customer-facing or internal processes. Addresses model complexity, opacity ("black box" risk), and the challenge of validating models that evolve post-deployment.

Implication

Any AI/ML system that influences a consequential business decision — credit, fraud, pricing, compliance — is now unambiguously within scope. Institutions that have treated ML models as tools rather than models will need to bring them into the formal model inventory, validation program, and governance framework.

NEW Tiered model risk classification framework ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required proportional oversight — more scrutiny for higher-risk models — but provided no standard taxonomy for risk tiering. Classification was left entirely to institution discretion, creating significant variation in practice.

2026 Revision

Introduces a structured three-tier classification: (1) High — models with significant financial, consumer, or regulatory impact; (2) Moderate — standard production models; (3) Low — simple, well-understood, limited-impact tools. Each tier carries defined validation frequency, documentation, and governance requirements.

Implication

Tiering reduces ambiguity and will be a primary examination focus. Examiners will assess whether institutions have applied tiering consistently and whether high-tier designations are capturing the right models — particularly AI/ML systems that may have been under-classified.

MODIFIED Expanded model inventory requirements ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required institutions to maintain a model inventory, but did not specify content requirements. In practice, inventories varied widely — some comprehensive, others little more than a list of names.

2026 Revision

Mandates specific inventory data fields: model name and purpose, risk tier, business owner, model developer, last validation date, next scheduled validation, key assumptions, known limitations, performance monitoring status, and — critically — whether the model is vendor-supplied or developed in-house.

Implication

Institutions with informal or spreadsheet-based inventories will face pressure to formalize. The explicit requirement to flag vendor models is significant — it sets the stage for heightened scrutiny of third-party model governance and vendor concentration risk.

02

Model Development & Implementation

NEW Algorithmic fairness and bias testing requirements ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

No explicit fairness or bias requirements. Fair lending compliance existed in parallel regulatory frameworks but was not integrated into MRM guidance.

2026 Revision

Requires bias testing and disparity analysis for models used in consumer credit, pricing, or other decisions with fair lending implications. Mandates documentation of data sourcing decisions — particularly where proxy variables for protected characteristics may be present. Requires adverse impact analysis as part of model development for in-scope models.

Implication

This provision bridges MRM and fair lending compliance, historically managed in separate silos. Model risk officers will need closer coordination with fair lending counsel and compliance teams. AI/ML credit scoring models face the highest scrutiny given their opacity and potential to encode historical disparities.

MODIFIED Data quality and lineage documentation — substantially expanded ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required developers to document data inputs and verify data quality. Relatively high-level — focused on ensuring inputs were appropriate and representative.

2026 Revision

Mandates full data lineage documentation — source, transformation steps, cleaning decisions, and known data gaps. Requires explicit assessment of training data representativeness for ML models, including vintage and coverage considerations. Addresses concept drift — the risk that a model trained on historical data becomes less predictive as the environment changes — and requires monitoring plans to detect it.

Implication

Concept drift monitoring will be a new operational requirement for most institutions. Models trained pre-pandemic and not revalidated post-pandemic are a primary examiner target — the economic discontinuity exposed the fragility of historically-trained models.

RETAINED Core requirement: document purpose, assumptions, and limitations ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required documentation of model purpose, design rationale, key assumptions, data inputs, and known limitations. Emphasized that developers must understand their own model's mechanics.

2026 Revision

Retains all core documentation requirements. Extended to explicitly cover AI/ML architectures — requiring that even complex models be documented at a level sufficient for a knowledgeable validator to assess design choices independently.

Implication

The "black box" defense is gone. Institutions cannot claim a vendor model is too complex to document. If a model cannot be adequately documented, it may not be appropriate for the intended use.

03

Model Validation

MODIFIED Validation independence standards — strengthened and clarified ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required effective challenge and independence — validators must be separate from model developers. The standard was qualitative; "effective challenge" was principles-based.

2026 Revision

Clarifies that independence means organizational separation with independent reporting lines, not merely different individuals. Explicitly prohibits validators from validating models they had material input in developing. Introduces the concept of "shadow validation" for high-tier models — parallel validation by a second independent team for models with systemic importance.

Implication

Community banks and smaller institutions that rely on the same small team for development and validation face the sharpest compliance challenge. The guidance acknowledges resource constraints and allows compensating controls — but these must be explicit and documented, not assumed.

NEW Vendor model validation — explicit and enforceable obligations ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Addressed vendor models only briefly, noting that institutions remain responsible. In practice, many institutions accepted vendor validation documentation as a substitute for independent review — a practice examiners tolerated inconsistently.

2026 Revision

Makes clear that vendor-supplied models must be independently validated by the institution, commensurate with risk tier. Vendor documentation may inform — but does not replace — independent validation. Institutions must have contractual rights to access model documentation, and validation findings must be shared with vendors for remediation where applicable.

Implication

This is the provision most likely to drive immediate operational change. Institutions that have accepted SOC reports and vendor white papers as validation substitutes will need to build independent validation capacity for their vendor model portfolio — or accept that high-tier vendor models carry elevated, documented residual risk.

NEW Continuous monitoring as a validation component for high-tier models ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required periodic validation on a defined cycle, supplemented by ongoing performance monitoring. Monitoring was a separate function from validation — periodic revalidation was the primary control.

2026 Revision

For high-tier models — particularly AI/ML models in production — requires continuous or near-continuous performance monitoring as an integral component of the validation function. Monitoring metrics must be pre-specified, with defined thresholds that trigger escalation to full revalidation. Addresses model drift, performance degradation, and population shift.

Implication

The line between validation and monitoring has effectively blurred for high-tier models. Model validation functions will need technology infrastructure — dashboards, automated alerts, statistical process controls — not just periodic review workflows. This is a meaningful resource investment for most institutions.

04

Governance, Oversight & Culture

MODIFIED Board and senior management responsibilities — materially elevated ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Required board and senior management to establish and maintain a model risk management framework, approve risk appetite, and receive periodic reporting on model risk. Responsibilities were framed at the governance structure level.

2026 Revision

Significantly elevates board-level expectations: boards must understand the institution's AI/ML model landscape, not just receive summary reporting. Senior management is responsible for ensuring that model risk expertise exists — not just that policies are in place. Introduces the concept of a Model Risk Committee at the management level as a governance expectation for larger institutions.

Implication

Board directors who lack technical literacy around AI/ML face increased scrutiny. Institutions should assess whether board composition and training programs are adequate — and whether board reporting has evolved beyond dashboard summaries to substantive model risk discussion.

NEW Model risk culture as an explicit supervisory dimension ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Did not address organizational culture explicitly. Governance was assessed structurally — committees, policies, reporting — not behaviorally.

2026 Revision

Introduces model risk culture as an explicit governance dimension. Examiners will assess: whether model limitations are openly communicated or suppressed; whether business lines push back on validation findings; whether the institution rewards model development velocity over model risk discipline; and whether model risk concerns reach senior management without distortion.

Implication

Culture is notoriously difficult to examine — but examiners have tools: interview patterns, escalation logs, instances where model limitations were disclosed to users, and cases where model findings conflicted with business preferences. This provision gives examiners latitude to raise concerns that structural governance metrics alone might miss.

RETAINED Three lines of defense model risk framework ▾
SR 11-7 (2011)

Implicitly supported a three-lines structure: business line model owners (first line), independent model validation (second line), and internal audit (third line). Did not use "three lines" terminology explicitly.

2026 Revision

Explicitly adopts three-lines-of-defense framing. Clarifies that internal audit should independently assess the MRM framework — not just review model inventories — and should have sufficient AI/ML expertise to do so meaningfully. Internal audit that lacks technical capacity to evaluate AI governance is itself a governance finding.

Implication

The audit finding implication is significant. Many internal audit functions have not invested in data science or ML expertise, relying on control framework reviews rather than technical model assessment. The 2026 guidance signals that this gap is itself a supervisory concern.
