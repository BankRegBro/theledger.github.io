What the bill does

H.R. 941 amends the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) at Section 704B — the provision added by Section 1071 of Dodd-Frank — to make three targeted statutory changes to the CFPB's small business lending data collection framework. It does not repeal the underlying data collection requirement; it narrows who must comply and adds a mandatory compliance runway with a penalty-free grace period.

The bill responds directly to the CFPB's 2023 final rule (88 Fed. Reg. 35150, May 31, 2023), which was subject to sustained industry litigation and multiple compliance-date extensions. By writing its key thresholds into the statute rather than regulation, H.R. 941 would constrain the CFPB's regulatory discretion on coverage and timing.

Legislative context

H.R. 941 is one of two 119th Congress bills targeting Section 1071. The companion bill, H.R. 976 (1071 Repeal to Protect Small Business Lending Act), would repeal ECOA §704B entirely. The Small LENDER Act takes the narrower approach of preserving the data collection mandate while adjusting its scope and providing compliance relief. Meanwhile, the CFPB issued its own proposed rule in November 2025 that independently proposed many overlapping changes at the regulatory level.

Statutory target

The bill amends Section 704B of the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (15 U.S.C. 1691c–2), which was added to ECOA by Section 1071 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010). The CFPB implements this authority through Regulation B, Subpart B (12 C.F.R. §1002).

ECOA §704B(g) — new paragraph (4)
Compliance timeline and penalty safe harbor
Adds mandatory 3-year compliance period + 2-year no-penalty grace period to the statute
View:
[No comparable provision exists in current ECOA §704B(g). The subsection grants the CFPB general rulemaking authority to "prescribe such rules and issue such guidance as may be necessary to carry out, enforce, and compile data pursuant to this section." No statutory compliance timeline or safe harbor is specified — those terms are set entirely by CFPB rulemaking.]
"(4) COMPLIANCE WITH COVERED RULE.— "(A) IN GENERAL.—With respect to the covered rule, the Bureau shall provide a financial institution a 3-year period beginning on the date the covered rule was issued to comply with the rule. "(B) SAFE HARBOR.—After the end of the 3-year period described under subparagraph (A), the Bureau shall provide a 2-year safe harbor to a financial institution during which the financial institution is required to comply with the covered rule but is not subject to any penalties for failure to comply with the covered rule. "(C) COVERED RULE DEFINED.—In this paragraph, the term 'covered rule' means the final rule of the Bureau titled 'Small Business Lending Under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (Regulation B)' (88 Fed. Reg. 35150, published May 31, 2023)."
Effect: Codifies a 5-year total runway (3-year compliance + 2-year no-penalty) running from May 31, 2023, placing the hard compliance start at approximately May 31, 2026 and extending penalty-free status through May 31, 2028. The CFPB could not shorten this timeline without a statutory amendment.
ECOA §704B(h)(1)
Definition of "financial institution"
Raises and codifies the loan origination threshold triggering data collection obligations
View:
"(1) FINANCIAL INSTITUTION.—The term 'financial institution' means any partnership, company, corporation, association (incorporated or unincorporated), trust, estate, cooperative organization, or other entity— "(A) that engages in any financial activity; and "(B) that originated not less than 25 credit transactions for small businesses in each of the preceding 2 calendar years. [Note: The 2023 CFPB final rule raised the regulatory threshold to 100 originations. The statute itself did not specify a numeric threshold — Congress delegated that determination to the CFPB.]
"(1) FINANCIAL INSTITUTION.—The term 'financial institution' means— "(A) any partnership, company, corporation, association (incorporated or unincorporated), trust, estate, cooperative organization, or other entity that engages in any financial activity; and "(B) in each of the previous 2 calendar years originated not less than 500 credit transactions for small businesses."
Effect: Raises the coverage threshold from the CFPB's regulatory level of 100 transactions to a statutory floor of 500 transactions and embeds it in ECOA itself. The CFPB could raise the threshold further by rule, but could not lower it below 500 without a statutory change. Lenders below 500 originations annually are completely exempt as a matter of law.
ECOA §704B(h)(2)
Definition of "small business"
Redefines "small business" by codifying a $1M gross annual revenue ceiling in the statute
View:
"(2) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term 'small business' has the same meaning as the term 'small business concern' in section 3 of the Small Business Act (15 U.S.C. 632). [Note: The 2023 CFPB rule set the threshold at $5,000,000 in gross annual revenues, departing from the SBA's industry-specific size standards in favor of a uniform revenue cap.]
"(2) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term 'small business' means any entity with gross annual revenues of $1,000,000 or less in the most recently completed fiscal year."
Effect: Replaces the SBA cross-reference (and the CFPB's $5M regulatory threshold) with a flat $1M revenue cap written into the statute. Businesses above $1M in revenues fall outside the data collection regime as a matter of law — the CFPB cannot expand coverage to larger businesses without congressional action.
1
Mandatory compliance runway — 3 years from rule issuance
The bill requires the CFPB to provide every covered financial institution a full 3 years from the date the 2023 final rule was issued (May 31, 2023) before requiring compliance. This is a statutory floor — the Bureau cannot shorten it. Running from the rule's publication, the compliance window would extend to approximately May 31, 2026. This codification prevents the CFPB from compressing the timeline in future rulemakings or reversing prior deadline extensions.
2
Two-year penalty-free safe harbor after compliance begins
After the 3-year compliance period, the bill provides a 2-year safe harbor during which institutions must technically comply with the rule but face no penalties for failure to do so. This effectively creates a 5-year total ramp-up period (through approximately May 2028) before enforcement exposure arises. The safe harbor is universal — it applies to all covered institutions, not only smaller lenders.
3
Coverage threshold: 500 originations per year (statutory floor)
The bill writes a 500-transaction origination threshold directly into ECOA. Under the 2023 CFPB rule, coverage began at 100 originations (per year, for 2 consecutive years). By placing 500 in the statute, the bill makes that floor a congressional mandate. Lenders below 500 small business originations annually are exempt as a matter of law — the CFPB cannot by rule expand coverage downward without congressional action. The CFPB retains discretion to set a higher threshold by rule.
4
Small business defined as ≤ $1M gross annual revenues
The bill eliminates the current SBA cross-reference definition and replaces it with a flat $1 million gross annual revenue ceiling, measured in the most recently completed fiscal year. The CFPB's 2023 rule set the cap at $5M; the agency's November 2025 proposed rule proposed lowering it to $1M by regulation. The bill would codify this $1M figure in the statute, meaning only loans to entities with $1M or less in annual revenues trigger collection obligations — removing a substantial segment of mid-market commercial lending from coverage.
5
Scope is limited to the 2023 final rule
The compliance timeline provisions (Provisions 1 and 2) are specifically tied to the CFPB's 2023 final rule (88 Fed. Reg. 35150) by name and Federal Register citation. They do not automatically extend to any replacement or revised rule the CFPB might issue. The definitions in Provisions 3 and 4, however, are written into ECOA §704B(h) generally and would apply to any future rules implementing Section 1071.

Key analytical distinctions

Bill vs. CFPB NPRM — overlapping outcomes, different legal durability. The November 2025 CFPB proposed rule and H.R. 941 converge on the same $1M small business revenue threshold. But H.R. 941 would entrench that figure in the statute, insulating it from future regulatory revision. The CFPB's proposed rule, if finalized, could later be amended or reversed by a future administration. The bill provides durability the regulatory route cannot.

Origination thresholds diverge. The bill sets 500 as a statutory floor; the CFPB's November 2025 NPRM proposed raising the regulatory threshold to 1,000. If both were enacted, the CFPB's 1,000-transaction threshold would govern (it is higher) — but the Bureau could never lower it below 500 without legislation.

The bill narrows more surgically than H.R. 976. The companion bill (H.R. 976) would repeal ECOA §704B entirely, eliminating the CFPB's data collection authority. H.R. 941 leaves the underlying mandate intact but constrains its scope and timeline — a more defensible position against fair lending objections and more likely to survive Senate scrutiny.

Compliance timeline provisions are rule-specific. The 3-year compliance period and 2-year safe harbor are tied by citation to the 2023 final rule only. If the CFPB were to issue a new or replacement rule, the statutory clock would not restart automatically — a potential drafting gap if the 2023 rule is superseded by regulation before enactment.
