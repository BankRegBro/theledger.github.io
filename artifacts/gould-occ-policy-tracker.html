Comptroller Jonathan V. Gould — Policy Action Tracker

32nd Comptroller of the Currency  ·  Confirmed July 10, 2025  ·  Actions through May 2026

Gould has moved aggressively since confirmation to reset the OCC's supervisory posture — reducing regulatory burden on community banks, aggressively defending and expanding NBA preemption authority, and opening the door to fintech and digital asset charters under the GENIUS Act framework. This tracker organizes his major actions across three policy pillars.

Gould has made reducing regulatory burden on community banks a centerpiece of his tenure, targeting examination frequency, BSA/AML compliance costs, CRA burden, capital requirements, and the politically charged use of "reputation risk" in supervision.

Jul 14, 2025 Disparate impact removed from Fair Lending handbook Final ▼
The OCC removed references to disparate impact liability from the Fair Lending booklet of the Comptroller's Handbook and instructed examiners to no longer examine for disparate impact. This was among Gould's earliest post-confirmation actions and was followed by similar moves at the FDIC (August 2025) and NCUA (September 2025). The change narrows fair lending examination to intentional discrimination and removes disparate impact as a supervisory tool.
Narrows fair lending examination; removes disparate impact as an examiner basis for criticism
Oct 6, 2025 Risk-based examination tailoring for community banks Final ▼
The OCC removed fixed examination requirements and shifted to risk-based scoping and frequency — reducing the supervisory footprint for community banks that pose lower risk. Gould framed this as preserving the value of the federal charter while eliminating unproductive reporting requirements. Applies to all OCC-supervised community banks (generally institutions with assets under $30 billion).
Reduces examination burden; frequency and scope now proportionate to each institution's risk profile
Oct 7, 2025 Proposed rule: eliminate reputation risk from supervision Final (Apr 2026) ▼
Jointly with the FDIC, the OCC proposed — and finalized in April 2026 — a rule prohibiting criticism or adverse action against banks based on "reputation risk." The rule explicitly bars regulators from encouraging debanking of customers on the basis of political, social, cultural, or religious views, or lawful business activity. Gould stated reputation risk "has been used as a pretext for decisions that have nothing to do with safety and soundness." Effective June 6, 2026.
Eliminates a longstanding examination category used to pressure banks on politically disfavored customers
Nov 24, 2025 Tailored BSA/AML examination procedures for community banks Guidance ▼
The OCC issued supplementary guidance calibrating BSA/AML examination procedures to community banks' generally low levels of money laundering and terrorist financing risk. Concurrently, data collection requirements on community banks were reduced. Gould has identified BSA/AML compliance as a key area where banks feel "put between a rock and a hard place" by examiners — facing potential criticism whether they file SARs or not.
Reduces BSA/AML compliance costs for lower-risk community banks; cuts data reporting requirements
Dec 17, 2025 Simplified CRA strategic plan for community banks Proposed ▼
The OCC proposed a simplified CRA strategic plan option enabling community banks to tailor their CRA examination to local credit needs, community capacity, product offerings, and business strategy. The proposal provides clearer guidance on measurable goals and reduces the complexity of CRA planning for smaller institutions. Gould stated community banks "by definition are focused on meeting the financial needs of their communities."
Enables community banks to demonstrate CRA performance on their own terms rather than one-size-fits-all metrics
Mar 19, 2026 Capital framework modernization proposals Proposed ▼
At the FDIC board meeting, Gould supported two proposals to modernize the regulatory capital framework for banking organizations of all sizes. The OCC estimates an aggregate 6.9% reduction in minimum binding capital requirements under the standardized approach for OCC-supervised banks, and a 3.4% reduction for the largest institutions under the expanded risk-based approach. The proposals also simplify capital calculations by eliminating parallel risk-weight methodologies.
Increases lending capacity; partial rollback of post-2008 Basel III capital requirements

A central Gould priority has been defending — and proactively expanding — NBA preemption authority, following adverse First and Ninth Circuit rulings on state escrow-interest laws. His approach combines formal rulemaking, amicus engagement, and a political coalition strategy described as returning the OCC to its early-2000s posture of aggressive preemption defense.

Oct 7, 2025 Statement supporting state bank preemption parity Policy signal ▼
In an unusual move, Gould issued a statement as FDIC board member explicitly supporting state-chartered bank preemption rights — noting that 1997 Riegle-Neal Act amendments put state banks on parity with national banks for preemption of host state laws, and that §27 of the FDI Act provides interest rate preemption. He encouraged the FDIC to take additional steps to support these congressionally-granted rights. This acknowledged that preemption benefits are not exclusive to the federal charter.
Notable: signals OCC support for state bank preemption rights — relevant to CSBS and state supervisors seeking parity
Nov 2025 Three-part preemption defense strategy announced Policy signal ▼
Speaking at the Clearing House annual conference, Gould laid out a three-part strategy in response to adverse First Circuit (Conti v. Citizens Bank) and Ninth Circuit (Kivett v. Flagstar Bank) rulings finding the National Bank Act does not override state escrow-interest laws: (1) proactively file amicus briefs defending preemption regulations; (2) work with Treasury to update preemption regulations; and (3) mobilize industry and political allies to protect the national bank charter. He described rebuilding preemption's "political legitimacy" as essential.
Sets strategic framework; first comprehensive OCC preemption defense posture since early 2000s
Dec 30, 2025 Proposed preemption determination: state escrow interest laws (12 states) Proposed ▼
The OCC published two concurrent NPRs. The first codifies national banks' power to establish mortgage escrow accounts and exercise business judgment over their terms — including whether to pay interest. The second is a formal preemption determination under Dodd-Frank §25b concluding that the National Bank Act preempts New York's interest-on-escrow law (Gen. Oblig. Law §5-601) and the substantively equivalent laws of 11 other states. The OCC characterized federal preemption as "a critical tool for reducing unnecessary burden, enabling local and national prosperity, and unleashing economic growth." Comments closed January 29, 2026.
Would eliminate mandatory escrow interest for national banks across 12 states; ongoing litigation context remains uncertain
Ongoing 2025–26 Amicus briefs in escrow preemption litigation Active ▼
The OCC has filed amicus briefs in active First and Ninth Circuit cases arguing that state escrow interest and interchange fee restriction laws can disrupt uniform management of deposit-related services and justify federal preemption under the Barnett Bank standard. Courts have thus far rejected the OCC's position, citing Dodd-Frank's requirement for case-by-case preemption analysis and the Supreme Court's Cantero v. Bank of America framework. Outcome remains contested.
Outcome uncertain; appellate courts have not deferred to OCC post-Cantero; formal rulemaking may be more durable path
Jan 12, 2026 NPRM: trust bank charter — non-fiduciary activity authority Proposed ▼
The OCC issued an NPRM to amend 12 C.F.R. §5.20 to clarify that national banks chartered as trust companies may engage in non-fiduciary activities in addition to fiduciary activities. The OCC characterized this as codifying a "well settled" point. This has significant implications for novel trust charter applicants — including fintech and digital asset firms — seeking broad operating authority under a national trust bank charter.
Expands functional scope of trust bank charters; directly facilitates stablecoin and fintech charter strategies

Gould has cleared the path for non-traditional and fintech bank charters at a pace not seen in recent years — granting conditional approval to Erebor Bank (the first new fintech bank of the Trump era) and positioning the OCC as the primary federal regulator for stablecoin issuers under the GENIUS Act.

Oct 15, 2025 Conditional charter approval: Erebor Bank, N.A. Approved ▼
The OCC granted conditional approval for the national bank charter of Erebor Bank, N.A. (Columbus, Ohio), a fintech startup associated with defense technology entrepreneur Palmer Luckey and backed by Peter Thiel. The Wall Street Journal described it as the first new bank approved in the Trump 2.0 era. The FDIC approved deposit insurance on December 16, 2025. Erebor was positioned as a tech-forward replacement for Silicon Valley Bank. The approval drew scrutiny from Sen. Elizabeth Warren, who raised concerns about the bank's fundraising memo citing its "political network" and "unique connectivity to banking regulators" including Comptroller Gould.
First fintech national bank charter under Gould; signals open-door posture toward novel charter applicants
Jan 7, 2026 World Liberty Financial stablecoin trust bank application Under review ▼
World Liberty Financial (WLF) — co-founded by President Trump and his sons, and issuer of the USD1 stablecoin — submitted an OCC application for a "national trust bank purpose-built for stablecoin services." Under the GENIUS Act (signed in summer 2025), the OCC is the primary regulator of federally licensed stablecoin issuers. Sen. Warren requested Gould delay review of the application given the President's financial interest in the applicant; Gould previously declined to answer "hypothetical questions" about WLF. The application remains under review.
High-profile test case for OCC stablecoin charter authority under the GENIUS Act
2025–26 GENIUS Act implementation: OCC as stablecoin primary regulator Active ▼
Congress passed the GENIUS Act in summer 2025, designating the OCC as the primary federal regulator for federally licensed stablecoin issuers. Gould has highlighted GENIUS Act implementation as a central 2025–26 priority in congressional testimony, positioning the OCC to charter and supervise both bank and non-bank stablecoin issuers. The OCC's trust charter clarification (January 2026 NPRM) is directly relevant to structuring stablecoin operations under a national trust bank charter.
Dramatically expands OCC's jurisdiction into digital asset issuance; competes with state money transmitter licensing regimes
Sep 2025 Debanking enforcement targeting digital asset firms Active ▼
Gould announced that the OCC — pursuant to President Trump's executive order on fair banking access — was investigating the largest national banks for potentially unlawful debanking of digital asset companies and other lawful businesses. The OCC stated it would use the licensing process, CRA exam process, consumer complaints, and third-party databases to surface debanking. OCC-supervised banks were put on notice they would be held accountable for unlawful debanking practices.
Signals OCC as an ally of crypto and fintech firms seeking account access; ongoing investigation of largest national banks
Mar 26, 2026 FSOC nonbank designation: OCC supports rolled-back framework Position taken ▼
At the March 2026 FSOC meeting, Gould issued a statement supporting a proposed framework reverting to a more limited approach to nonbank SIFI designation. Drawing on his own experience representing MetLife during FSOC's prior designation effort, Gould called the prior approach "among the most egregious abuses of government power" he had witnessed in 25 years. The OCC's support for a constrained FSOC framework reduces systemic risk label pressure on large fintech firms and non-bank financial companies.
Reduces regulatory exposure for large fintechs that might otherwise face bank-like systemic oversight
